History Of Traditional Mask

when it comes to culture, Sri Lanka is doubtlessly unique. Different parts of this island nation has mastered different types of arts for the last thousand years and the southern part of the island has many arts and history contained. the Southern dance style, the Southern musical instruments, even the way they fish is an art that no other part in the island follows. City of Ambalangoda- the city of masks, is legendary for creating and using traditional Sri Lankan masks.

The history of Sri Lankan masks dates back to the 15th century, with strong ties to devil worship. For anyone interested in the art of Sri Lankan mask carving and masquerade dances, a trip to Ambalangoda is a must. Here you will find the Mask Museum : a museum, workshop and library dedicated to everything related to masks.

The Sri Lankan mask suggests dramatic and varied meanings to the visual image, hopes and imagination arising from the beliefs and fears of rural folk . These are considered part of religion, ritual dramas and folk theater with ritual overtones.

Types of Traditional Masks in Sri Lanka – Island Secrets

Mask carving has been practiced for generations and is a folk art piece that has stood the test of time. Here are the top six mask types on the island, ranging from devil masks to divine masks.

  1. Raksha Masks
  2. Garuda Masks
  3. Kolam Masks
  4. Sanni Masks
  5. Makara Masks
  6. Maha Kola Masks

Raksha Masks

Perhaps the most colourful and fearful looking of the masks, the Raksha Marks or the Demon Masks as they are widely known are commonly featured in Raksha dance rituals. There are five main varieties of Raksha masks. They are the Naga Raksha Mask or Cobra Mask, the Gurulu Raksha Mask, Maru Raksha Mask (associated with Death), Purnaka Raksha Mask and the Rathnakuta Raksha Mask.

Garuda Masks

Often regarded as one of the more unique mask varieties in Sri Lankan folk art, the Garuda Mask has a rich history steeped in Hindu mythology and spirituality. As the vehicle of Lord Vishnu, the Solar Bird as it is known is employed in the Kolam tradition to ward off or battle with the cobra demon. Elaborate and frightening in appearance the Garuda Mask typically features defeated cobras which the sacred bird wears as trophies of past victories.

Kolam Masks

Kolam performances are a variety of plays that are used not only to entertain but also to re-enact diverse mythologies and folk beliefs of villagers. They are used in traditional works of theatre.

Kolam mask makers are few and far between these days, yet the Ariyapala Wijesuriya clan continues to build masks and perform Kolam dance rites. Kolam masks have a long history dating back to King Maha Sammatha's reign. According to legend, his wife, the queen, felt compelled to watch a masked dance performance while pregnant, despite the fact that such dance forms were unknown to the local public at the time. Sanni Masks

It is mostly used to treat illnesses. Sanni masks come in 18 different variations, each one representing a different bodily illness ranging from small pox to fits and fevers. The origin of these afflictions is consequently assigned to a specific demon, who is represented as a masked dancer at the Sanni performance. The patient is made to see the dispelling of these disease-causing demons, or Sannis, in order to alleviate not just the disease's physical symptoms, but also the illness's physiological hold on the patient.

Makara Masks

The Makara Mask is the representativae mask for Sri Lanka's fishing communities. It is usually used to denote persons who belong to the Karava ethnic group. The Makara Mask is carved on pliable wood species such as Balsa wood and depicts a woman juggling spheres with her hands. When the mask user is moving, a wire mechanism is employed to move this part up and down. Visitors staying at The River House, a beachfront boutique hotel in Sri Lanka, can visit a mask workshop along the coastal belt to learn more about the folk art form.

Maha Kola Masks

The Maha Kola Mask has a special position in folk art and the Sanni heritage as the primary mask of the Devil Dance. The Maha Kola Mask wearer is a trusted figure in folk traditions entrusted with the curing of the patient by the family. Worn by the "medicine man" or the dancer who portrays the heroic figure who dispels the illness from the patient by destroying the demon possessing the body, the Maha Kola Mask wearer is a trusted figure in folk traditions entrusted with the curing of the patient by the family.
